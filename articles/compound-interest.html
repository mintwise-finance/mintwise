What Is Compound Interest? The 8th Wonder of the World

Mar 18, 2026 6 min read

There is a famous quote often attributed to Albert Einstein: "Compound interest is the eighth wonder of the world. He who understands it, earns it... he who doesn't... pays it."

Compound interest is the snowball effect applied to money. It is the primary mathematical reason why the rich get richer and why early investors can retire millionaires.

Simple vs Compound Interest

Imagine you invest $1,000 and earn 10% interest every year.

With Simple Interest, you earn $100 every year based on your original $1,000. After 10 years, you've made $1,000 in interest.

With Compound Interest, you earn interest on your interest. In year one you have $1,100. In year two, you earn 10% on $1,100 (which is $110, not $100). Year three you earn 10% on $1,210. The snowball has started.

The Power of Time

Let's look at a real-world example to see why time is more important than money.

Person A starts investing $300 a month at age 25. They stop completely at age 35. They invested $36,000 of their own money in total. They never invest another dime, but leave the money to compound at an 8% return.

Person B waits until age 35 to start investing. They also invest $300 a month at an 8% return, but they keep doing it every single month until they turn 65 (30 straight years). Person B invested $108,000 of their own money.

At age 65, who has more money?

Shockingly, Person A wins. Person A will have around $615,000, while Person B will have around $440,000. Person A invested one-third of the money, but because they gave it 10 extra years to compound, their snowball grew much larger.

How to Capture Compound Interest

You don't need a math degree to use this. You just need three things: Capital (money), Return (investing in stocks or ETFs), and Time. The earlier you start, the less money you actually have to invest out of your own pocket to build massive wealth.
