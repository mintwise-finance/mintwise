FHA vs Conventional Loans for First-Time Homebuyers

Mar 18, 2026 9 min read

The single biggest myth stopping young people from buying real estate is the phrase: "You need a 20% down payment to buy a house." While 20% guarantees you lower monthly costs, the vast majority of first-time buyers use FHA or Conventional loans that require between 3% and 5% down.

The Conventional Mortgage (3% - 5% Down)

Conventional loans are regular mortgages issued by private banks. If you have a solid credit score (620 or higher, ideally 740+), you can often secure a conventional loan for as little as 3% down.

The Catch: Because you didn't put down 20%, the bank sees you as a higher risk. They will charge you PMI (Private Mortgage Insurance). This adds between $50 and $200 to your monthly payment. However, the beauty of a conventional loan is that once you pay off enough of your house (reaching 20% equity), the PMI gets canceled automatically.

The FHA Loan (3.5% Down)

An FHA loan is a mortgage backed by the Federal Housing Administration. It is specifically designed to help people with lower credit scores (as low as 580) or higher debt-to-income ratios buy homes.

The Catch: The FHA loan requires MIP (Mortgage Insurance Premium). Unlike conventional PMI that drops off, FHA insurance lasts for the entire 30-year life of the loan. The only way to get rid of it is to completely refinance your house into a conventional loan later on.

Which One Should You Choose?

If your credit score is above 680, a Conventional loan is almost always the mathematically superior choice because the Private Mortgage Insurance is cheaper and can eventually be canceled without the cost of refinancing.

If your credit score took a hit recently (between 580 and 650), the FHA loan is often the only realistic pathway. Use it to lock in your house, spend the next few years improving your credit, and refinance to a Conventional loan later.
