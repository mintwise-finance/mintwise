Renting vs Buying a Home: The Real Math

Mar 18, 2026 10 min read

Society tells us that "renting is throwing money away" and "buying a house is the ultimate financial goal." But if you actually run the numbers, you'll discover that buying a home isn't always the right financial move, and renting isn't just setting money on fire.

The Hidden Costs of Owning a Home

When comparing rent to a mortgage, people often compare $2,000 in rent to a $2,000 mortgage payment. This is a massive mistake. Your rent is the maximum you will pay for housing every month. Your mortgage is the minimum.

When you own a home, you also pay for:

  • Property Taxes: $3,000 to $10,000+ per year that never stops.
  • Homeowners Insurance: Typically much more expensive than renters insurance.
  • Maintenance: Roofs, HVAC systems, and plumbing break. Rule of thumb: budget 1% of the home's value per year for repairs.
  • Interest Payments: For the first 10 years of a 30-year mortgage, the vast majority of your payment goes to the bank as pure interest — not to paying down your house.

When Renting Makes You Richer

Renting can actually make you wealthier under certain conditions. If you rent a $1,500 apartment instead of buying a house that costs $2,500 per month (including taxes and maintenance), you have an extra $1,000. If you invest that $1,000 into the stock market every month, historical data shows your stock portfolio will likely outperform the housing market over a 20-year period.

When Buying is the Right Move

Buying a home makes financial sense if:

  • You plan to live in the same city for at least 7 to 10 years (because closing costs wipe out short-term profits).
  • You want stability and the freedom to drill holes in the wall.
  • You have a 20% down payment and an emergency fund left over.

The Verdict

Buying a house is a lifestyle choice that acts as a forced savings account. Renting buys you flexibility and zero maintenance headaches. Neither is intrinsically "right" — you must do the math for your specific city and situation before locking into a 30-year commitment.
