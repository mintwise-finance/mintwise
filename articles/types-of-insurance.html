5 Types of Insurance Everyone Should Understand

Mar 18, 2026 9 min read

Insurance isn't designed to make you wealthy. It's designed to keep you from becoming poor. One car accident, one medical emergency, or one house fire can wipe out a decade of savings if you aren't protected. Here are the five policies you need to know about.

1. Health Insurance (Mandatory)

A single trip to the emergency room without insurance can cost upwards of $3,000, and a surgery can easily bankrupt an average earner. Health insurance is non-negotiable. Whether you get it through your employer or the marketplace, you must have it. Understand your deductible (what you pay before insurance kicks in) and your out-of-pocket maximum.

2. Auto Insurance (Mandatory)

If you drive, you need auto insurance. But the bare minimum required by law (liability insurance) only pays for the other person's car if you cause an accident. If you want your own car repaired, you need Collision and Comprehensive coverage.

3. Renters Insurance (Highly Recommended)

Many young adults skip this, but it is the cheapest insurance you can buy — typically $10 to $15 per month. If your apartment burns down or gets robbed, your landlord's insurance covers the building, but it covers zero of your personal belongings. Renters insurance buys you new furniture, laptops, and clothes if disaster strikes.

4. Term Life Insurance (Situational)

Do you need life insurance? Ask yourself one question: "If I died tomorrow, would someone suffer financially?"

If you have a spouse, children, or a cosigned mortgage, you need Life Insurance. Term Life Insurance is generally the best choice because it is cheap and straightforward — you pay a low monthly premium for a set "term" (like 20 years), and if you die during that time, your family gets a large cash payout. Avoid "Whole Life" insurance unless you are exceptionally wealthy, as the fees are astronomical.

5. Disability Insurance (Hidden Gem)

You are far more likely to become disabled during your working years than you are to die prematurely. Long-term disability insurance replaces a portion of your income (usually 60%) if an injury or illness prevents you from working for months or years. Many employers offer this as a free benefit, so check your HR portal today.

The Golden Rule of Insurance

Insure the catastrophes (house fire, cancer, death, total loss of car) and self-insure the nuisances (a broken cell phone screen, a scratched bumper) through your emergency fund. This will save you thousands in premiums over your lifetime.
