What is a 401(k) and How Does Employer Matching Work?

Mar 18, 2026 8 min read

A 401(k) sounds like a confusing tax code, mostly because it literally is a confusing tax code (section 401, subsection k). However, in the real world, a 401(k) is simply a special type of investment account offered by employers that comes with two massive benefits: Tax savings and Free Money.

The Tax Advantage

When you put money into a traditional 401(k), the IRS pretends you didn't earn that money this year. For example, if you earn $60,000 and put $5,000 into your 401(k), the government only taxes you on $55,000. You get to keep more of your paycheck right now, and the money grows completely tax-free until you retire at age 59½.

The Employer Match (FREE MONEY)

This is the greatest deal in finance. Many companies offer a "401(k) Match" to encourage you to save. A common structure is "100% match up to 5% of your salary."

What does that mean? If you earn $50,000 a year, and you decide to invest 5% of your salary ($2,500) into the account, your boss will literally hand you another $2,500 for free and put it in the same account.

If you don't contribute that 5%, the boss keeps the $2,500. Not contributing up to the match is identical to walking past a pile of cash on the sidewalk and refusing to pick it up.

Mistakes to Avoid

  • Not Investing the Money: A 401(k) is just an empty basket. Once the money is inside, you must log into the portal and actually choose a fund (like a Target Date Fund or S&P 500 ETF) so it grows.
  • Cashing Out Early: If you withdraw money before age 59½, you will pay federal taxes, state taxes, AND a massive 10% penalty fee. Never touch it unless facing bankruptcy.
