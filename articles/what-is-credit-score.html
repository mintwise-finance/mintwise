What Is a Credit Score and Why Does It Matter?

Mar 18, 2026 6 min read

In the financial world, your credit score is your permanent record. It is a three-digit number ranging from 300 to 850 that tells lenders exactly how likely you are to pay back money you borrow.

Why Your Score Matters

A high credit score (740+) unlocks the best interest rates on mortgages, auto loans, and credit cards. A low score (under 600) means banks will charge you significantly higher interest — if they approve you at all. Over a lifetime, a bad credit score can cost you hundreds of thousands of dollars in extra interest.

How Your FICO Score is Calculated

The most common scoring model is the FICO score. It is broken down into five exact categories:

1. Payment History (35%)

This is the most important factor. Have you paid your past credit accounts on time? A single payment that is 30 days late can drop your score drastically.

2. Amounts Owed / Utilization (30%)

This looks at how much of your available credit you are using. If you have a credit card with a $10,000 limit and a $9,000 balance, your utilization is 90% (which looks terrible to banks). Keep this below 30% at all times.

3. Length of Credit History (15%)

The longer you've had credit accounts open, the better. This is why you should never close your very first credit card, even if you rarely use it, as long as it has no annual fee.

4. New Credit (10%)

Opening several new credit accounts in a short period of time represents risk to lenders. Every time you apply for a card, the bank does a "hard pull" on your report, which dips your score slightly.

5. Credit Mix (10%)

Lenders like to see that you can handle different types of debt (e.g., a credit card, a car loan, and a mortgage) safely.

How to Fix a Bad Score

Set up autopay for the minimum balance on all your cards so you never miss a payment. Pay down your existing balances to get your utilization under 30%. Then, just wait. Time and consistent on-time payments are the only true cures for bad credit.
